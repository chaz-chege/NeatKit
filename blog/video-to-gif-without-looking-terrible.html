How to convert a video to GIF without it looking terrible

Neatkit Team July 2026 7 min read

GIFs have a reputation for looking bad. Blurry motion, washed-out colours, harsh dithering patterns that make the image look like it was printed in a newspaper from 1987. Most video-to-GIF converters produce exactly this — and most people assume that's just what GIFs look like.

It isn't. A well-made GIF can look surprisingly good. The difference between a terrible GIF and a good one isn't the source video quality — it's the decisions made during conversion. This guide explains those decisions and how to make them correctly.

Why GIFs look bad by default

The GIF format was invented in 1987. It was designed for simple graphics on computer monitors that displayed 256 colours at most. The fundamental technical constraint built into the format reflects that origin: each frame of a GIF can contain at most 256 unique colours.

A typical video frame contains millions of distinct colour values. A sky gradient alone might have ten thousand different shades of blue. Compressing that into 256 colours requires discarding most of the colour information — and the result is visible banding and posterisation where smooth gradients used to be.

There's no way around this constraint. It's part of the GIF specification. The format hasn't been updated since 1989. Every GIF, no matter how it's made, is limited to 256 colours per frame. What differs between a good GIF and a bad one is how intelligently that 256-colour budget is spent, and how well the motion is handled within the frame rate limitations.

The colour palette problem — and how to solve it

256 colours sounds like very few because it is. The question is which 256 colours. A naive approach picks a fixed palette — the most common 256 colours across all frames of the GIF. This means every frame uses the same palette, which wastes the budget on colours that are important in some frames and irrelevant in others.

A better approach generates a different palette for each frame based on the colours actually in that frame. This is called per-frame paletting. It's more computationally expensive — instead of computing one palette, you compute one per frame — but it produces dramatically better results because each frame's colour budget is optimised for what's actually in it.

The best approach combines per-frame paletting with dithering. Dithering is a technique that simulates colours that aren't in the palette by alternating pixels of nearby palette colours. A grid of alternating blue and purple pixels, for example, appears as a blueish-purple when viewed at normal size. Dithering effectively extends the apparent colour range beyond 256 by exploiting the eye's tendency to blend adjacent colours.

The choice of dithering algorithm matters. Floyd-Steinberg dithering — the most common algorithm — diffuses the quantisation error from each pixel to its neighbours. It produces a characteristic speckled or grainy appearance that can look either natural (on photographic content) or messy (on graphics with flat areas). Sierra and Atkinson dithering produce slightly different visual results and may be better for specific content types. Most converters only offer one option. A good one lets you choose.

Frame rate: the biggest quality lever

Frame rate has more impact on GIF quality than almost any other setting, because it directly affects file size, and file size is what forces compromises on every other dimension.

A 5-second video at 30fps has 150 frames. Each frame, even compressed, contributes to file size. A GIF with 150 frames will be enormous — likely 20–50MB for typical content, which is too large for most use cases and too slow to play smoothly in most contexts.

Reducing to 15fps gives you 75 frames and roughly halves the file size. Reducing to 10fps gives 50 frames. At 10fps you start to notice the choppiness — motion looks slightly stuttery — but for slow-moving content (a person talking, a subtle animation) 10fps can still look acceptable.

The content of your video determines the minimum acceptable frame rate. A talking head at 10fps is fine. A fast-moving sports clip at 10fps looks terrible. Choose the lowest frame rate that still makes the motion feel smooth for your specific content, and use the file size savings to improve colour quality instead.

Resolution: the other major lever

GIF files scale linearly with the number of pixels. A GIF at 480p wide has roughly four times fewer pixels than a GIF at 960p wide, and will be roughly four times smaller at equivalent frame rate and quality settings.

For a GIF that will be displayed in a web browser or messaging app, 480–640 pixels wide is almost always sufficient. The GIF will be displayed at perhaps 400px wide in most contexts — displaying it at 960px source resolution wastes file size on resolution that gets downscaled away anyway.

The optimal resolution also depends on how much motion and detail is in the content. A close-up face with subtle expression changes needs higher resolution to capture the detail. A text animation that's mostly white background can be quite small without losing quality.

The lossy GIF trick

The GIF format doesn't have a quality setting — it's technically lossless within its 256-colour constraint. But there's a widely used technique that adds lossy compression to GIF by deliberately introducing patterns in the pixel data that the LZW compression algorithm (which GIF uses internally) can compress more efficiently.

This is sometimes called "lossy GIF" compression, and it's how Gifsicle's --lossy flag works. By strategically replacing pixel values with nearby palette colours in a pattern that compresses well, you can reduce GIF file sizes by 30–50% at a minor visible quality cost. The quality loss appears as subtle noise in areas of flat colour, which is often less noticeable than the compression artefacts you'd get from just reducing the palette or frame rate further.

When not to use GIF

For most purposes in 2025, GIF is the wrong format. WebP animations support millions of colours, proper video codecs, and alpha transparency (real transparency, not the 1-bit on/off transparency GIFs support). MP4 video is even more efficient. APNG (animated PNG) supports full colour and transparency without GIF's limitations.

GIFs persist because of compatibility. Every browser, every operating system, every messaging app, every email client displays GIFs correctly without any additional support. They autoplay without user interaction in almost every context. They loop infinitely by default. They're embeddable anywhere an image tag is accepted.

The cases where GIF is still the right choice: anywhere compatibility matters more than quality or file size, messaging apps that support images but not video (though most major apps now support MP4), email (where video autoplay is unreliable), and any context where you can't guarantee video playback support.

For everything else — social media posts, website animations, Discord, Slack — WebP or MP4 will give you better quality at smaller file sizes. The only reason to use GIF is when something specifically needs a GIF.

Practical settings for a good GIF

For a 5-second clip that needs to look good and be under 5MB:

  • Resolution: 480px wide (maintain aspect ratio)
  • Frame rate: 12–15fps for moderate motion, 8–10fps for slow content
  • Palette: Per-frame, 256 colours
  • Dithering: Floyd-Steinberg for photographic content, no dithering for graphics with flat colours
  • Lossy compression: 30–50 (on tools that offer it)

Always preview before downloading. The settings above are starting points — your specific content will need adjustments. A clip with lots of colour variation needs more palette depth. A slow pan needs fewer frames. The preview lets you see what the tradeoffs actually look like before committing to a download.
