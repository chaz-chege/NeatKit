How to trim a video in your browser without installing anything

Neatkit Team July 2026 6 min read

Trimming a video used to require installing a video editor. That's not true anymore. Modern browsers can trim video files locally — no upload, no account, no software to install — and for the most common use case (cut the start or end off a clip), the result is instant and lossless.

This guide explains how browser-based video trimming actually works, when it produces a perfect result and when it doesn't, and what the quality tradeoffs actually look like.

Two completely different things called "trimming"

The word "trim" hides an important distinction that determines everything about quality and speed. There are two fundamentally different operations that both look like "cutting video":

Lossless trim (also called stream copy) cuts the video at specific points without re-encoding the video data. The frames between your cut points are kept exactly as they are, copied directly to the new file. No quality is lost because no re-encoding happens. The operation is extremely fast — trimming a 1GB video takes a fraction of a second because the processor isn't doing any video decoding or encoding work.

Re-encode trim (also called transcode) decodes the entire video, then re-encodes only the portion you want to keep. The result is frame-accurate — you can cut at any exact frame — but the re-encoding introduces generation loss (the quality of the output depends on the encoding settings), and the operation takes significant time because every frame is processed.

Most people don't know this distinction exists. They just want to cut their video. But the distinction determines whether your trim takes 0.1 seconds or 3 minutes, and whether the output is identical to the original or slightly degraded.

Why lossless trim can't always cut exactly where you want

Lossless trimming has one significant limitation: it can only cut cleanly at keyframes.

Video files don't store every frame independently. That would be enormous — a 30fps video would need 30 complete images per second. Instead, video codecs store periodic keyframes (complete images) and then store subsequent frames as differences from the previous frame ("this frame is the same as the last one, except these 200 pixels changed"). This is how modern video codecs achieve their high compression ratios.

Keyframes typically appear every 1–5 seconds in most video files (the exact interval depends on the encoder settings). A lossless trim can only start cleanly at a keyframe because there's no preceding frame to reference for the in-between frames. If you try to start a lossless trim at frame 47 of a segment where the keyframe is at frame 40, the result will either start at frame 40 (not where you wanted) or the early frames will look corrupted because they're referencing a preceding keyframe that isn't in the new file.

For most use cases — "remove the first 10 seconds" or "cut out the last 30 seconds" — this doesn't matter. You're trimming large chunks, and whether the cut happens a half-second before or after your intended point is irrelevant. For precise editing — "this clip starts exactly at the 2:47:13 mark" — you need re-encoding.

How browser-based trimming works

FFmpeg is the software that powers almost all video processing, from Hollywood post-production to YouTube's transcoding pipeline. It's open source, highly capable, and has been compiled to run in WebAssembly, which means it can run inside a browser tab.

Neatkit's Video Trimmer uses FFmpeg.wasm — a WebAssembly build of FFmpeg that runs locally in your browser. When you trim a video, the FFmpeg process runs on your device's CPU. The video file is loaded into your browser's memory, FFmpeg processes it according to the trim parameters you set, and the output is written to memory for you to download.

Nothing is uploaded. The network tab in your browser's developer tools will show no outbound file transfer. You can verify this by disconnecting from the internet after the page loads — the trim still works.

The tool offers both lossless and re-encode modes. Lossless is the default because it's instant and produces perfect quality for the common use case. Re-encode mode is available when you need frame-accurate cuts.

Performance on different devices

Lossless trimming is effectively instant regardless of file size or device — it's copying bytes, not processing video. A lossless trim of a 2GB video file takes under a second on any modern device.

Re-encoding is a different story. FFmpeg.wasm running in a browser doesn't have access to hardware video acceleration (GPU encoding), so it uses software encoding on the CPU. This is slower than native hardware encoding by a factor of 5–10×. On a modern laptop or flagship phone, a 1-minute video re-encodes in approximately 30–90 seconds. On older devices or low-end Android phones, the same operation might take 5–10 minutes.

The speed also depends on the output codec and quality settings. H.264 at medium quality is faster to encode than H.265. Lower resolution encodes faster than higher resolution. If you're doing a re-encode trim and performance is slow, reducing the output resolution (720p instead of 1080p) can cut encoding time significantly.

Format compatibility

FFmpeg.wasm supports a wide range of input formats: MP4, MOV, MKV, AVI, WebM, and most other common video containers. For lossless trimming, the output format is the same as the input — you get an MP4 out if you put an MP4 in.

A few formats require special handling. MKV files with certain codec combinations may not support lossless stream copy correctly — in these cases, re-encoding is the reliable option. Very old AVI files sometimes have timing metadata that causes issues with lossless trimming. When in doubt, re-encode.

When you need a proper video editor instead

Browser-based trimming is excellent for the simplest case: remove the beginning, remove the end, save the middle. If what you need is more complex than that, a dedicated tool is the right choice:

  • Multiple cuts from within a clip: Removing specific sections from the middle of a video, keeping disconnected parts, or creating a highlight reel requires a timeline editor.
  • Adding titles, music, or transitions: Any compositing operation is outside the scope of a trimmer.
  • Frame-accurate precision editing: Professional editing, documentary work, or anything where sub-second timing matters needs dedicated software with proper timeline scrubbing.
  • Very long files on low-end devices: Processing a 10GB video file in a browser tab on a 2GB RAM phone will cause memory issues. Native software handles this better.

For casual use — "cut out the first 15 awkward seconds of this video before I send it to someone" — the browser tool handles it instantly and without any account or installation. That covers the vast majority of why people want to trim a video.

The practical workflow

Open the trimmer, drag in your video file, set the start and end points on the timeline, choose lossless if the cuts don't need to be frame-precise (they usually don't), and download. For a standard trim operation, the whole process takes under a minute regardless of file size. The output file is ready to send, post, or use immediately — no encoding wait, no quality loss, no upload.
