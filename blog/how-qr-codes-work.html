How QR codes actually work — and how to make one that doesn't expire

Neatkit Team July 2026 7 min read

QR codes are everywhere — restaurant menus, product packaging, bus stops, event tickets, business cards. Most people scan them without thinking much about what's inside. Fewer people generate them, and most of those who do use a random online generator without understanding what they've actually created or how long it will work.

This guide explains the mechanics of how QR codes store data, why some QR codes stop working when a company goes out of business, and how to generate a QR code that will still scan in ten years.

What a QR code actually is

A QR code (Quick Response code) is a two-dimensional barcode. Where a traditional barcode encodes data in the width of parallel lines, a QR code encodes data in a grid of black and white squares arranged in a specific pattern. The grid can encode text, URLs, contact information, or any other string of characters.

The data is encoded using a combination of error correction and binary representation. The squares are arranged into modules, and the pattern of filled and empty modules represents binary data. Error correction is built into the standard: even if up to 30% of a QR code is obscured or damaged (depending on the error correction level you choose), it can still be decoded correctly. This is why you can put a logo in the middle of a QR code and it still scans.

The important technical point is that the data encoded in a QR code is fixed at generation time. Once the QR code image is created, the data inside it cannot be changed. The only way to change what a QR code points to is to generate a new QR code.

Static vs dynamic QR codes — the distinction that matters

Here's where most people get confused, and where the business model of QR code generators creates a real problem.

A static QR code encodes your data directly. If you want a QR code that points to https://neatkitapp.com, the characters of that URL are encoded directly into the QR code's pixel pattern. Scan it with any QR reader, anywhere, at any time, and it decodes to that URL. The QR code works as long as the image file exists. It has no dependency on any external service.

A dynamic QR code doesn't encode your destination URL directly. Instead, it encodes a short redirect URL hosted by the QR code generator's own servers — something like https://qr.generatorservice.com/abc123. When you scan the code, your phone goes to their server, which then redirects you to your actual destination. This gives you the ability to change the destination after printing (because you're just updating the redirect on their server), and it lets the generator service track scan analytics.

The catch is obvious once you see it: if the QR code generator shuts down, stops paying for their domain, or decides to put the redirect feature behind a paywall, your QR code breaks. Not because anything changed about the code itself — but because the intermediary it depends on is gone.

This has happened repeatedly. QR code generators have shut down, been acquired, changed their free tier, or simply let their redirects go dead. Every printed menu, business card, or poster that used a dynamic code from those services now scans to an error page.

How the data is actually encoded

Understanding the encoding helps you understand the size limits and why some QR codes are denser than others.

QR codes come in versions 1 through 40. Version 1 is a 21×21 grid, version 40 is a 177×177 grid. Each version can store more data. The maximum capacity depends on the data type and error correction level:

  • Numeric data only (digits 0–9): up to 7,089 characters at the highest version
  • Alphanumeric (letters, numbers, some symbols): up to 4,296 characters
  • Binary/byte data (arbitrary characters, including full URLs): up to 2,953 characters

A typical URL like https://neatkitapp.com/tools/qr-generator is 42 characters — well within any version's capacity. A long URL with tracking parameters can be several hundred characters, requiring a higher QR code version and producing a denser, harder-to-scan image. This is why URL shorteners are commonly used with QR codes — not just for aesthetics, but to keep the QR code simpler and easier to scan reliably.

Error correction has four levels: L (7% recovery), M (15%), Q (25%), H (30%). Higher error correction makes the QR code more robust against damage but also larger and denser. For most purposes, level M or Q is the right choice — enough resilience for real-world use without making the code unnecessarily complex.

Why most QR code generators are problematic

Most free QR code generators online use dynamic codes by default. The ability to track scan analytics — how many people scanned, from where, on which device — is the feature they sell. The dynamic redirect is how they collect that data. Scan tracking requires their servers to be in the middle of every scan.

If you're a business that genuinely needs scan analytics, a paid dynamic QR service makes sense. For personal use, event tickets, business cards, or anything that needs to work reliably over time, static codes are almost always the right choice.

There's another problem with most generators: they watermark the QR code image, produce low-resolution PNGs that look bad when printed large, or put usage limits on free generation. None of these limitations are technical necessities — they're commercial choices.

What to encode for permanent QR codes

If you want a QR code that genuinely never expires, you need to encode data that you control permanently:

  • A URL on your own domain: https://yourdomain.com/menu will work as long as you keep the domain. You can change where that page points to whenever you want.
  • Plain text: Contact details, a Wi-Fi password, a short message. No URL means no dependency on any service staying online.
  • A vCard: Contact information in the vCard format encodes directly into the QR code without any external service.
  • Wi-Fi credentials: The WIFI: format lets you encode a network name and password directly into a QR code. Scan it and your phone connects automatically. No server involved.

What to avoid for permanent use: any shortened URL from a third-party service (bit.ly, tinyurl.com), any redirect from a QR generator's domain, or any URL that depends on a platform you don't control.

QR code design and scannability

A few practical notes on making QR codes that actually scan reliably in the real world:

  • Minimum size: 2.5cm × 2.5cm (1 inch square) for scanning at arm's length. Smaller codes work in controlled conditions but fail in real-world lighting and camera angles.
  • Quiet zone: QR codes require a border of white space (at least 4 module widths) around all sides. Placing a QR code too close to other design elements reduces scan reliability.
  • Contrast: Black on white is the most scannable combination. Dark colour on light colour works. Avoid light on dark — most QR readers expect the standard orientation.
  • Logo embedding: Logos placed in the centre of a QR code work because of error correction. Keep the logo under 30% of the total QR code area and use error correction level H. Test with multiple devices before printing.
  • Always test before printing: Generate, print a test copy at the actual size it will appear, and scan with three different devices before you order 500 business cards.

SVG vs PNG for QR codes

Most generators offer PNG output. For print use, SVG is almost always better. SVG is a vector format — it scales to any size without losing quality. A QR code saved as SVG can be printed at billboard size with perfect sharpness. A QR code saved as PNG at 300×300 pixels will be blurry and unscannable when printed larger than a business card.
