How to compress a video for WhatsApp Status (and why it keeps rejecting your clip)

Neatkit Team July 2026 7 min read

WhatsApp Status videos have a 16MB file size limit. WhatsApp voice notes have their own ceiling. Sent videos get compressed further by WhatsApp's own encoding. And none of this is explained anywhere in the app — you just get a generic error, or the video goes through looking terrible.

This guide explains exactly what WhatsApp is checking, why your video fails, and how to compress it correctly without buying software or signing up for anything.

What WhatsApp actually checks when you upload a video

When you try to send a video or post it to Status, WhatsApp runs several checks in sequence. Most people think there's just a file size limit. There are actually four distinct gates your video has to pass:

  • File size: 16MB for Status, 16MB for direct messages (though this varies by platform and has changed over time). This is the limit most people know about.
  • Duration: WhatsApp Status videos are capped at 30 seconds. A file under 16MB that's 45 seconds long will still be rejected.
  • Codec compatibility: WhatsApp accepts H.264 video and AAC audio in an MP4 container. If your video uses a different codec — H.265/HEVC, VP9, AV1 — it may be rejected or silently re-encoded with quality loss.
  • Resolution and frame rate: Very high resolution videos (4K, 1080p at 60fps) that are under 16MB can still cause issues on lower-end devices WhatsApp is optimising for.

The 16MB limit is the most common problem, but codec incompatibility is a silent killer. Your video goes through, but it looks noticeably worse because WhatsApp re-encoded it on the fly using settings you didn't control.

Why your video is probably too large

Modern smartphones shoot video at resolutions and bitrates that were considered professional quality a decade ago. A one-minute video shot at 1080p on a recent Android phone is typically 150–300MB depending on the phone's codec and quality settings.

iPhone shoots in HEVC (H.265) by default from iPhone 7 onwards, which produces smaller files at equivalent quality compared to H.264. But WhatsApp handles HEVC inconsistently across platforms, so an iPhone video that looks fine when sent iPhone-to-iPhone may look degraded when received on Android, because WhatsApp re-encoded it.

The gap between what your phone records and what WhatsApp accepts is large. A 30-second clip at 1080p is typically 50–80MB. You need to get it to under 16MB — roughly a 4× to 5× reduction — without it looking unwatchable.

What compression actually does to your video

Video compression works by reducing the amount of data used to represent each frame. There are two main levers: resolution and bitrate.

Resolution is the number of pixels in each frame. 1080p has roughly 2 million pixels per frame. 720p has about 900,000. Dropping from 1080p to 720p doesn't just halve the file size — it reduces data by about 56% before you've touched anything else. For WhatsApp, 720p is usually the right target. At phone screen sizes, the difference between 720p and 1080p is invisible.

Bitrate is how many bits are used to represent each second of video. A higher bitrate means more detail is preserved but a larger file. For a 30-second WhatsApp Status under 16MB, you want a total bitrate of around 3,500 kbps (video + audio combined). Most phone cameras record at 15,000–40,000 kbps, so you're reducing the bitrate by 4× to 10×.

The visible quality impact of bitrate reduction depends on your video content. Static or slow-moving footage — a landscape, a conversation — compresses well and looks good at lower bitrates. Fast motion — sports, action, rapid panning — needs more bitrate to avoid blocky artefacts.

The WhatsApp Status target: what you're actually aiming for

Here are the settings that reliably produce a good-looking WhatsApp Status video:

  • Codec: H.264 (not HEVC)
  • Resolution: 720p (1280×720) or lower
  • Video bitrate: 2,500–3,000 kbps
  • Audio bitrate: 128 kbps AAC
  • Container: MP4
  • Duration: Under 30 seconds
  • Target file size: Under 14MB (leave 2MB headroom for WhatsApp's own processing)

Why 14MB rather than 16MB? WhatsApp sometimes applies its own light compression pass when uploading, which can push a 15.8MB file just over the limit. A 14MB target gives you a buffer.

When WhatsApp rejects it anyway

If your compressed video is under 16MB but WhatsApp still won't post it to Status, the most likely culprits are duration (check it's under 30 seconds), aspect ratio (some unusual ratios cause issues — 16:9 is safest), or a codec that slipped through. Check the video file details on your device — look for codec information — and make sure it shows H.264, not HEVC or AV1.

Another common issue: WhatsApp on Android and iOS sometimes behave differently with the same file. If a video fails on Android but works on iOS, the issue is usually codec handling. Re-encoding with the H.264 settings above resolves it in almost every case.

WhatsApp direct messages vs Status

Sending a video directly to someone in a chat is slightly different from posting to Status. The 16MB limit applies, but there's no 30-second duration cap. WhatsApp will also compress videos you send in chats on its own — they don't arrive at full quality. If you want someone to receive a video at the quality you shot it, don't send it through WhatsApp chat. Send it through a file-sharing link instead.

For Status, the compression has to happen before you upload, because WhatsApp's own compression on Status posts is aggressive. A 30MB video posted to Status will come through to viewers at around 5–8MB quality — visibly degraded. Compressing it yourself first, to around 12MB at good settings, gives you control over where the quality loss happens and how it looks.

The longer-term fix

If you post videos to WhatsApp Status regularly, the real solution is to record in a more WhatsApp-friendly format from the start. Most Android phones let you change the video recording resolution in the camera settings. Setting it to 720p by default produces files that are 4–5× smaller and go through WhatsApp with minimal compression loss. You sacrifice detail you couldn't see on a phone screen anyway.

For iPhones, going to Settings → Camera → Formats and switching from HEVC to H.264 resolves most codec compatibility issues at the cost of slightly larger original files.
