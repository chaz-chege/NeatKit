The problem with AI detectors — and what this one does differently

Neatkit Team July 2026 9 min read

AI detectors have a credibility problem. They give confident-sounding percentages for things they can't actually know with certainty. They've been used to penalise students who wrote their own work. They flag non-native English speakers at higher rates than native speakers. And the best ones — the ML-based commercial tools — won't tell you why they reached a conclusion, only that they did.

This article is an honest account of what AI detectors can and can't do, why the problem is harder than it looks, and what Neatkit's detector does differently — including the things it can't catch.

Why AI detection is genuinely difficult

The core problem is that AI-generated text and human-written text are not two separate categories with a clean boundary. They're overlapping distributions of writing patterns.

Formal academic writing — the kind produced by a careful human scholar — tends to be structured, use precise vocabulary, employ consistent sentence lengths, and avoid colloquial expressions. These are also characteristics of AI writing. A non-native English speaker who learned formal writing through grammar instruction produces text that looks, statistically, very similar to what a language model produces — because the grammar instruction was itself creating formal, structured, predictable patterns.

Conversely, a language model prompted to "write casually" will use contractions, vary sentence lengths, add colloquialisms, and produce text that looks, statistically, more like human writing. The surface features shift based on the prompt.

This means any detector based on writing style signals is measuring the wrong thing. It's not measuring "was this written by an AI" — it's measuring "does this look like the kind of writing an AI typically produces." Those are different questions, and they have different answers for different types of text.

How commercial detectors actually work

Turnitin, GPTZero, and Originality.ai use machine learning models trained on large datasets of labelled text — documents known to be human-written versus documents known to be AI-generated. The model learns the statistical features that distinguish the two categories and applies those patterns to new text.

These models are more sophisticated than keyword lists or simple pattern matching. They operate on features that aren't visible to the human eye — token probability distributions, perplexity scores, subtle rhythmic patterns in sentence structure. They're also proprietary black boxes: you get a score, not an explanation.

The limitations are well-documented even for these sophisticated tools. In internal testing and independent academic research, false positive rates of 5–20% are common — meaning 5–20% of genuinely human-written texts are flagged as AI. For a tool being used to make academic integrity decisions, that's a serious problem. A classroom of 30 students submitting original work might see 2–6 false positives from a single detector.

The accuracy problem is also worsening over time. When GPT-3.5 was the state of the art, the detectors trained on its outputs were reasonably accurate at detecting it. GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini write differently — more naturally, with fewer of the surface tells that earlier models had. The detectors are constantly playing catch-up with models that are actively being trained to produce more natural-sounding text.

What signals actually matter — and which have aged out

The early generation of AI text had obvious tells. It overused transition words: "Furthermore," "Moreover," "It is important to note that." It produced sentences of eerily uniform length. It reached for abstract nouns when concrete ones would do: "the implementation of the solution" instead of "how we solved it." It avoided first-person. It never used contractions.

These patterns were real in 2022–2023. They're much weaker signals now. Frontier models in 2025 are explicitly trained to avoid these tells. A well-prompted Claude or GPT-4 output won't have "Furthermore" every other paragraph. It will vary sentence lengths. It can write in first person, use contractions, and produce text that's statistically similar to natural human writing on all the surface features the early detectors relied on.

What still has signal — though weaker than before:

  • Abstract noun density: AI still tends to over-nominalize — turning verbs and adjectives into abstract nouns. "The implementation of the optimization" where a human would write "how we optimized it."
  • Human voice markers: Genuine contractions, em dashes used naturally, first-person anecdotes, colloquialisms, rhetorical questions, parenthetical asides — these are reliably human, and their absence is still diagnostic.
  • Sentence length distribution: Human writing has higher variance. Very uniform sentence lengths remain an AI signal, though less so than before.
  • Structural patterns: AI still tends toward clean paragraph structure, balanced section lengths, and summary conclusions. Human writing is messier.

What Neatkit's detector does differently

Most detectors present a score and no explanation. Neatkit's detector shows you exactly which sentences triggered which signals and why. The sentence-by-sentence breakdown is not a presentation layer on top of a black-box score — it's the actual detection mechanism. The score is an aggregate of what the sentence analysis found.

This transparency has a concrete benefit: it's useful even when the overall score is wrong. If a human academic writer gets a high score, the breakdown shows which specific patterns triggered it — overuse of abstract nouns, absence of contractions, very uniform sentence structure. That's actionable information about writing style, regardless of whether AI was involved.

The signals Neatkit uses are calibrated to 2025 writing patterns, not 2022 ones. The detector doesn't heavily weight "furthermore" and "moreover" anymore — it weights human voice markers and structural patterns that remain diagnostic even for frontier model output. This makes it less likely to fire on formal human writing that happened to use transition words, and more sensitive to the absence of genuine human voice.

It's also honest about its limitations. The FAQ on the tool page states plainly that no detector can reliably distinguish well-written AI text from human writing, and that the results should be used as a writing quality signal rather than a verdict.

The privacy argument for client-side detection

Turnitin and GPTZero require you to upload the text you want to check. That text goes to their servers, is processed by their models, and — depending on their terms — may be retained and used for model training or comparison against future submissions.

For an educator checking student work, this means student work is being sent to a third-party service. For an individual checking their own writing before submission, it means their unpublished text is on someone else's server. For a company checking internal documents, it means potentially proprietary content is in third-party infrastructure.

Neatkit's detector runs entirely in the browser. The text never leaves your device. The analysis runs in JavaScript using pattern matching and statistical calculations. There's nothing to send, and nothing is sent. This doesn't affect the accuracy — the same signals work regardless of where the computation happens — but it removes the privacy exposure entirely.

What this tool should and shouldn't be used for

Use it for: getting a sense of whether a piece of writing has AI-like characteristics, identifying specific patterns in your own writing that read as formulaic, and as one signal among several when evaluating writing authenticity.

Don't use it for: making definitive judgments about whether specific text was AI-generated, academic integrity decisions without other corroborating evidence, or flagging non-native English speakers' writing as suspicious.

The honest position on AI detection in 2025 is that it's a probabilistic signal with known false positive and false negative rates, calibrated to current AI writing patterns that are actively shifting. A high score suggests AI-like writing characteristics. It doesn't prove AI involvement, and it can't. Any tool that claims otherwise is overselling what detection can do.
