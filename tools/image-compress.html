Quick tips for best results

80% quality is the sweet spot for most photos — invisible quality difference, roughly half the file size. Drop to 60–70% for web thumbnails where speed matters more than sharpness.
Convert PNG → WebP for the biggest size reduction. WebP is 25–35% smaller than JPEG at the same quality and supported by all modern browsers.
Use Max Width to simultaneously resize and compress. A phone photo at 4000px wide served on a website that displays it at 800px is wasting 80% of its data — cap it.

How browser-based compression works

Most online image compressors upload your file to a server, process it remotely, then send it back. This tool skips all of that. It uses the HTML5 Canvas API built into your browser to decode, redraw, and re-encode your image locally — the same rendering engine that powers browser-based image editors like Photopea.

When you adjust the quality slider, the browser re-encodes the image using its built-in JPEG or WebP codec at the specified compression level. Lower values tell the codec to discard more high-frequency detail (fine textures, sharp edges) that the human eye is less sensitive to — resulting in a smaller file with a visual quality that's often indistinguishable from the original.

JPEG vs PNG vs WebP — which should you use?

Choosing the right format before compressing can make a bigger difference than the quality slider itself.

📷
JPEG — photos and gradients
Best for photographs, product images, and anything with smooth colour gradients. Lossy compression. Doesn't support transparency. The universal format for sharing photos.
🖼️
PNG — logos and screenshots
Best for logos, icons, screenshots, and images with sharp edges or transparency. Lossless — no quality loss. But files are much larger than JPEG for photos.
⚡
WebP — web use
Best for websites and apps. Supports both lossy and lossless compression, handles transparency, and produces files 25–35% smaller than JPEG. Use this for anything going online.
