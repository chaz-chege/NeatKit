Tips for better QR codes

Higher error correction = more scannable. Use Level H (30% recovery) for QR codes that will be printed on textured surfaces, merchandise, or anywhere that might get worn or dirty. Use Level L for clean digital displays where scan speed matters more.
Test before printing. Always scan your QR code with multiple phones before committing to a print run. Test in different lighting conditions and at different distances. If it scans reliably on three different devices, it'll work in the field.
Size matters. The minimum printable size for a QR code is about 2×2cm (0.8×0.8in) for most phone cameras. For outdoor billboards or large posters, scale proportionally and ensure there is always a quiet zone (white border) of at least 4 modules around the code.

How QR codes work

QR (Quick Response) codes are two-dimensional barcodes that encode data in a grid of black and white squares. They were invented in 1994 by Denso Wave for tracking car parts in manufacturing and have since become universal for linking physical objects to digital content.

The code is structured into several regions: finder patterns (the three large squares in corners that help cameras locate and orient the code), alignment patterns (smaller squares in larger codes that help correct for perspective distortion), timing patterns (alternating black/white stripes that define the grid), and the data region (the actual encoded information with error correction redundancy).

🔗
URL / Website
The most common use. Encodes a full URL including https://. When scanned, the phone's camera app opens the link directly in the browser. Keep URLs short — shorter data means simpler QR codes that scan faster.

Error correction levels explained

QR codes use Reed-Solomon error correction to remain scannable even when partially damaged or obscured. Higher error correction adds more redundancy — meaning the code can lose more of its data and still decode correctly, but also makes the code visually denser.

LevelRecovery capacityBest for
L — LowUp to 7% damageClean digital displays, screens, PDF documents
M — MediumUp to 15% damageGeneral purpose, most printed materials
Q — QuartileUp to 25% damageIndustrial use, labels, product packaging
H — HighUp to 30% damageMerchandise, outdoor prints, textured surfaces, logo overlay

Where people use QR codes

🍽️
Restaurant menus
Table QR codes linking to a digital menu. Update the menu content without reprinting codes — as long as the URL stays the same, the QR code doesn't change.
📇
Business cards
A vCard QR code on your business card lets people scan and save your contact instantly. No typing, no spelling errors. Works even without internet access.
🛍️
Product packaging
Link to product instructions, warranty registration, how-to videos, or a reorder page. Use error correction Level H for packaging that may be handled, folded, or stored in humid conditions.
📣
Marketing materials
Flyers, banners, posters, and billboards linking to landing pages, social profiles, or promotions. Use a URL shortener before generating so the QR is simpler and scans faster.
🎫
Event tickets
Each ticket gets a unique QR code encoding the booking reference or ticket ID. Scannable at entry without printing full ticket details — just the code and the attendee name.
📶
WiFi sharing
Print a WiFi QR code and stick it near your router, in your Airbnb, or at your office reception. Guests scan and connect without you having to read out the password every time.

PNG vs SVG — which format to download?

🖼️
Download PNG
Best for: WhatsApp, social media, email, presentations, anywhere you're inserting an image. PNG is a raster format — fixed resolution. Download at the largest size you need, since scaling up reduces quality.
📐
Download SVG
Best for: print design, business cards, large format printing, Illustrator/Inkscape/Figma. SVG is vector — infinitely scalable with no quality loss. Always use SVG for anything that will be printed.
